Price vs.(intrinsic) value of a stock

  • The price of a stock is the amount of money we pay when we buy one unit of the stock or we receive when we sell one unit of stock.
  • The value of a stock is how much the stock is worth (recall the formula for calculating the intrinsic value you learned in financial management course).
  • The price of a stock is often roughly the same for buyers who want to buy and sellers who want to sell if the stock is heavily traded.
  • But value of a stock may be different to different investors. This is why there are trades in the stock market.
  • An investor buys a stock when she thinks it is undervalued and sells it when she thinks it is overvalued.
  • Data of a stocks price is the core component of equity (fundamental and technical) analysis.

Import stock price data

  • We first learn how to obtain and analyze raw stock price data (section 2.1).
  • Then we learn the techniques that help us to manipulate raw stock price data (clean the data) to fit some basic analysis (section 2.2 and 2.3).
  • A stocks price data can be downloaded from some websites, for example, Yahoo Finance.
  • Two ways to download them:
  1. Download the data directly with csv file from the website.
  2. Use a specific R function, such as getSymbols from package quantmod (convenient but may not be reliable, so will not be used).

Check the ticker symbol before you download the stock data

  • Be careful about the ticker symbol you use.
  • Sometimes you think the ticker symbol is for the stock you want to analyze, but actually it is not.
  • For example, in Yahoo Finance:
    1. Hewlett-Packards: HPQ not HP.
    2. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company Limited: TSM not TSMC
    3. S&P500 index: ^GSPC not SPX
    4. Dow Jones Industrial Average: ^DJI not DJIA
    5. NASDAQ: ^IXIC not NAS
  • Notice that some of the data can not be downloaded (e.g., S&P500 and Dow Jones)

Step 4: Sort data into chronological order

  • Also be careful if you save the Yahoo Finance data into csv file by using Excel.
  • For example, in rare situations, data might appear in reverse chronological order.
  • To solve this, use
data_AMZN<-data_AMZN[order(data_AMZN$Date), ]
  • But before using the function order, be careful about class of the column of Date (It should be Date class).
